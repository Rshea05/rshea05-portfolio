Enterprise UX Systems

Building an enterprise design system is rarely a design problem. It is an operating model, tooling, budget, and adoption problem.

Role Lead UX Strategist

Led the strategy for governance, shared patterns, accessibility, adoption, and cross-product design operations.

Tooling leadership Acting PM for UX platform change

Coordinated tool evaluation, stakeholder alignment, access paths, rollout needs, and budget tradeoffs.

Outcome Scalable UX operating model

Improved consistency, accelerated collaboration, reduced duplication, and lowered platform costs.

The ask sounded like a component problem. The reality was an operating model problem.

The initial assumption was that Bayer needed a design system. Discovery quickly showed the problem was much larger.

Across the organization, multiple product teams had created their own component libraries, interaction patterns, and brand implementations. Designers were deeply embedded within individual product groups, creating tribal knowledge that made it difficult to move between teams or contribute across products. Branding requirements frequently conflicted with accessibility standards, resulting in inconsistent user experiences and duplicated effort throughout the organization.

Instead of asking "What components are missing?" we reframed the question to "Why hasn't a shared system emerged?"

Disconnected systems

Product teams had local libraries, one-off workflows, and inconsistent interaction patterns.

Brand versus WCAG friction

Brand standards and accessibility needs frequently created tradeoffs without shared decision rights.

Adoption risk

A new library would fail unless teams had incentives, support, and ownership in the system.

Discovery focused on behaviors and decision rights, not just UI inventory

The team audited existing products, tools, libraries, brand implementations, accessibility gaps, and team behaviors. Interviews and workshops included designers, PMs, engineers, research partners, and product leadership.

Audit products and libraries Interview designers, PMs, and engineers Surface governance blockers Map duplicated patterns Define contribution model Pilot with product teams Expand adoption

Workshop artifact

Workshop outputs helped the team align without exposing sensitive product details

Whiteboard sessions gave designers, PMs, engineers, and leaders a shared place to sort patterns, ownership questions, exceptions, and adoption blockers. For portfolio use, the artifact is intentionally abstracted so it communicates the working method without revealing internal content.

The system needed enterprise consistency with intentional flexibility

Rather than creating another design library, we defined the initiative as an enterprise governance problem.

The goal became establishing a shared foundation that every product team could adopt without removing the flexibility needed for unique business requirements.

Standardize

Core components, reusable interaction patterns, accessibility requirements, and documentation standards.

Govern

Contribution models, design champions, decision rights, and shared ownership across product teams.

Flex

Clear divergence rules for moments where product context, brand needs, or workflow complexity required variation.

One of the biggest lessons was recognizing that governance required ownership throughout the organization. Design champions were identified within each product group to provide feedback, advocate for adoption, and help evolve the system alongside the teams using it.

Governance became a network of ownership

The design system connected product teams, engineering, accessibility, brand, UX Ops, and design champions through feedback, contribution, standards, exceptions, and adoption loops.

System artifact

Governance became tangible through a shared component foundation

A Bayer-aligned component system made the operating model visible: shared navigation, buttons, tabs, list rows, fields, menus, drawers, states, and accessibility patterns that teams could adopt instead of rebuilding locally.

Rollout happened incrementally, not through a big-bang launch

Implementation happened incrementally rather than through a single launch.

As Bayer transitioned to a new design platform, the migration became an opportunity to standardize tooling alongside the design system itself. Rather than asking teams to change twice, both efforts were combined into a single organizational initiative.

The design system was continuously refined through workshops, usability sessions with designers, engineering feedback, and pilot implementations across multiple products.

This iterative approach allowed the system to mature while maintaining trust with product teams.

Tooling was part of the adoption strategy

I also acted as a PM for the UX platform transition: coordinating evaluation, clearing access paths for designers and partner teams, aligning stakeholders, and controlling for budget as the organization moved away from fragmented tools.

The toolchain made the governed path easier to follow

The most important criterion was choosing tools people wanted to use. Figma enabled shared libraries, component versioning, developer inspect, and live cross-team collaboration. UserZoom created a research environment with observation, recordings, shared notes, and more transparent stakeholder participation. Miro provided a common space for discovery workshops, journey maps, service blueprints, affinity mapping, and cross-functional planning.

The toolchain did not just support the system. It made the system easier to adopt.

The same systems thinking scaled discovery operations

As product teams scaled, UX Research and Strategy became a bottleneck. PMs needed to gather early signals, but they needed structure and guardrails so early discovery did not create low-quality inputs downstream.

Problem framing Assumptions and risks Interview intake Validation checklist Research ready Design ready

Templates created consistent inputs while quality gates protected the work before it moved into deeper research, UX strategy, or design. PMs handled early signal gathering and hypothesis framing; UX Research led deeper behavioral validation; UX Strategy and Design focused on system-level optimization.

The result was more than a component library

The result was more than a component library.

The organization established a scalable UX operating model that enabled designers to contribute across products, reduced duplication between teams, improved accessibility compliance, and created shared patterns that engineering could implement consistently.

The platform migration also reduced licensing costs significantly while maintaining the capabilities required by the design organization, demonstrating that governance improvements could create measurable operational savings alongside better user experiences.

Consistency

Shared patterns and reusable components reduced local reinvention across products.

Throughput

PM-led discovery templates and quality gates helped discovery scale faster than headcount.

Executive value

Tool consolidation paired adoption with measurable cost reduction and operational clarity.

What actually made the system work

A design system is not successful because it has great components.

It is successful because people choose to use it.

The most valuable investment was not building buttons or documentation. It was creating governance, identifying champions within product teams, and designing a system flexible enough that people wanted to adopt it instead of creating another one.

Great UX organizations do not just design products. They design how products get designed.
